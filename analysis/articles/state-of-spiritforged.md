# State of Spiritforged: What Competitive Players Need to Know

**Published:** February 11, 2026  
**Author:** Hextech Analytics  
**Launch Date:** February 13, 2026

---

Two days out from Spiritforged's launch, and the first real test of Riftbound TCG begins. Origins was the foundation—solid, safe, exactly what a base set should be. Spiritforged is where we find out if Riot can actually build a TCG ecosystem that evolves without collapsing under its own weight.

The stakes are simple: nail this expansion, and Riftbound has legs. Miss the mark, and we're looking at another TCG that fades after the novelty wears off.

Let's talk about what's actually in this set and what it means for competitive play.

## The Core: 221 Cards, Three New Mechanics

Spiritforged brings 221 new cards to the table—smaller than Origins, but strategically denser. This isn't a bloated expansion trying to do everything; it's a focused set built around a single mechanical throughline: equipment.

The set introduces three new keywords:

**Equip** is the headline act. Units can now wear gear, and equipment cards exist as permanents that buff your board. This isn't a cute side mechanic—it's the entire identity of the set. If you're playing Spiritforged cards, you're playing equipment.

**Gold tokens** create an economic resource outside the traditional Domain mana system. We're talking about an alternative path to power that doesn't rely on your Domain curve. Expect ramp strategies to emerge that leverage Gold to cheat out threats earlier than the mana curve suggests possible.

**Repeat** is the mystery box. Details are still thin, but any keyword that appears across multiple rarities and Domains matters. Based on preview patterns, this looks like a mechanic that rewards going wide or playing multiple instances of similar effects.

The thematic hook is Ionia during the Spirit Blossom festival—the veil between mortal and spirit realms has lifted, and warriors are forging new weapons to meet the moment. It's a strong narrative wrapper for what's ultimately a mechanical expansion about permanents and board presence.

## The Equipment Revolution

Here's what actually matters for competitive play: equipment permanents fundamentally change removal calculus.

In Origins, removal was straightforward. Opponent plays a unit, you answer with a spell or a bigger unit. Clean trades, predictable lines. Spiritforged throws gear into the mix, which means unit value isn't static anymore. That 3/3 your opponent just played? It's actually a 5/5 with Overwhelm once they slap equipment on it next turn.

Suddenly, removal timing matters. Do you burn your answer on the base unit, or wait to see what they Equip? Do you run gear destruction, or just accept that their threats get bigger? These are real strategic questions, and the decks that solve them first will dominate early meta.

The flip side: equipment creates inevitability. If your deck revolves around assembling units + gear, you're building toward a board state that's harder to answer than the sum of its parts. That's dangerous in a good way—it creates defined win conditions and forces interaction.

Expect the meta to split into two camps: decks that leverage Equip for overwhelming board presence, and decks that ignore equipment entirely and go under or over the top. There's no middle ground here.

## Champions Worth Watching

Spiritforged brings **12 new Champion Legends**. Five are already confirmed: **Ezreal** (The Prodigal Explorer), **Fiora** (The Grand Duelist), **Irelia** (The Blade Dancer), **Lucian** (The Purifier), and **Rumble** (The Mechanized Menace).

**Fiora and Rumble** anchor the two preconstructed Champion Decks at $20 each. These are your entry points if you're not cracking packs. The smart money says Fiora's deck leans into Equip synergies—she's *The Grand Duelist*, after all. Rumble likely brings Yordle synergy or a Blue/tech-focused build.

**Irelia** is the card to watch for competitive players. Blade Dancer screams "go-wide strategy," and if she has Equip synergies, she's potentially the centerpiece of an aggressive equipment deck. Irelia + cheap Equip cards = explosive turns that punish slow setups.

**Ezreal and Lucian** are wildcards. Ezreal's Prodigal Explorer title suggests spell velocity or card advantage. Lucian as The Purifier could be removal-focused or a midrange value engine. Both have competitive pedigrees in other Riot games—don't sleep on them.

The other seven legends are still being revealed, but here's the pattern to watch: Riot is pushing Domain diversity. "More ways to play each Domain" was the stated design goal, which means we're getting legends that enable new archetypes within existing color identities. That's healthy for the competitive ecosystem if execution is solid.

## What the Competitive Scene Looks Like

Pre-Rift events (prerelease) already happened February 6-12. If you attended, you got a feel for sealed Spiritforged gameplay. If you didn't, here's what you missed: equipment in limited is *strong*. Gear cards that enhance your best units create insurmountable advantages in formats with shallow card pools.

Constructed is a different beast. **Summoner's Skirmishes** kick off in March with promotional equipment cards as prizes, and **Regional Qualifiers** are already integrating Spiritforged into the 2026 competitive season. The timeline is aggressive—you've got less than a month to figure out the meta before it matters for points.

The biggest competitive story isn't Spiritforged itself—it's **Proving Grounds cards entering Western circulation**. Starting now, Nexus Night promo packs can drop **16 Rares and Epics from Proving Grounds**, the China-exclusive set that's been locked away from Western players since launch. This includes cards like foil Garen that were straight-up unavailable unless you imported or lived in China.

That's a massive accessibility shift, and it means the Western meta is about to absorb strategies that were previously off-limits. If you've been theorycrafting decks around Proving Grounds cards you couldn't get, that list just became viable. Start testing now.

China got Spiritforged on December 12, 2025—two months ahead of the Western launch. That head start matters, but not as much as you'd think. English-language coverage of the Chinese meta is sparse, and regional metagames evolve differently based on player culture and card availability. The Western meta will develop independently. Don't copy-paste Chinese decklists blindly; test your own lines.

## For Collectors: Overnumbered and Chase Cards

Spiritforged's Overnumbered pool is **30 cards**, with **12 having Signature variants**. That's a shift from Origins, where Overnumbered cards were almost exclusively Champion Legends. Now you've got gear and champion units in the Overnumbered pool, which spreads collection priorities across multiple card types.

Signature variants are the chase cards. If you're cracking packs for value, those 12 Signature Overnumbered cards are what you're hunting. Expect competitive staples with Signature treatments to hold value long-term.

Alternate art promos are equipment-focused for the Spiritforged cycle. Summoner's Skirmishes, Regional Qualifiers, and Nexus Nights all feature equipment cards with alternate art as prizes. If you're a completionist, you're grinding events. There's no shortcut.

The **drawing/lottery system** is new for Spiritforged. Riot's official merch store moved to a lottery model instead of first-come-first-served purchasing. Drawings opened February 3 at 9am Pacific. Community reception has been mixed—supply concerns are real, and it's unclear if demand will exceed what the lottery system can fulfill. If you didn't get into the drawing, you're hitting local game stores or the secondary market. Plan accordingly.

## The Bottom Line

Spiritforged is the inflection point. Origins proved Riot could make a functional TCG. This expansion proves whether they can iterate on it without breaking what worked or playing it too safe.

The equipment mechanic is bold. It's not a side gimmick—it's the entire set's identity. That focus is both a strength and a risk. If Equip gameplay is engaging and creates strategic depth, Spiritforged is a hit. If equipment feels mandatory or warps the meta into a narrow equipment-or-bust landscape, we've got problems.

Early signals are positive. The mechanical hooks (Equip, Gold, Repeat) create new deck-building space without invalidating Origins cards. Domain diversity is expanding, not contracting. The competitive calendar is integrated and aggressive. Proving Grounds accessibility is a gift to Western players who've been locked out of key cards for months.

The real test starts February 13. Week one decklists will tell us everything. Watch for equipment-centric aggro builds (Irelia likely leads the charge), Gold-ramp midrange strategies, and revised Origins archetypes that slot in Spiritforged disruption tools.

If you're playing competitively, your homework is clear: test equipment synergies, theory-craft removal strategies that account for gear permanents, and don't ignore Proving Grounds cards now that they're finally accessible. The meta is about to shift hard.

Welcome to Spiritforged. Let's see if it sticks.
